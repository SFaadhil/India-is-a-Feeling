Discover Pune

Pune is Maharashtra's cultural capital — a city where ancient Maratha history lives alongside modern campuses, thriving tech parks, and a coffee culture that never sleeps. Whether you're exploring Shaniwar Wada, trekking Sinhagad Fort, or diving into the buzzing Koregaon Park café scene, every corner of Pune pulses with energy.

For exchange participants, Pune offers the rare combination of a cosmopolitan, English-friendly environment and deep-rooted Indian traditions. The city's large student population makes it welcoming, dynamic, and endlessly enriching.

City Highlights

What makes Pune unforgettable.

Shaniwar Wada

The great Peshwa fort that once commanded the Maratha Empire stands now as a ruin — the interior burned in 1828, leaving only the massive stone walls and ornate entrance gate. The son et lumière show held on weekend evenings narrates the fall of the empire with considerable atmospheric effect. It is the most historically significant site in Pune and worth an evening regardless of the performance.

Sinhagad Fort

Rising almost 1,300 metres above the Sahyadri pass, Sinhagad — the Lion's Fort — is the site of the 1670 battle where Tanaji Malusare died taking the fort from the Mughals in a night assault. The climb from the base takes about 40 minutes; the view from the ramparts across the Khadakwasla reservoir and the Deccan plateau is worth the effort. The local bhakri and white butter sold at the top have been feeding climbers for generations.

Koregaon Park

Pune's most cosmopolitan address is a long, tree-lined street where the Osho Meditation Resort, independent bookshops, craft cafés, and designer boutiques share space in a low-rise neighbourhood that has somehow remained pleasant while everything around it accelerated. It has been home to Pune's most internationally mobile residents since the 1980s. For exchange participants, it is the most natural base for social life outside the university campuses.

Lavasa City

Carved into a forested amphitheatre of hills in the Western Ghats, Lavasa was designed to look like an Italian hill town — pastel waterfront buildings, pedestrian plazas, and a lake. The project ran into legal and financial difficulties that have left it partially complete; the result is strange, quietly atmospheric, and worth a half-day from Pune. The drive through the Ghats to reach it is consistently beautiful.

Puranpoli & Misal

To eat in Pune is to understand Maratha culture — the sweet jaggery-laden puranpoli appears at every festival; the Puneri misal pav, served dry and fiercely spiced, is the city's most characteristic everyday dish. Xaviers in Viman Nagar and the stalls of Mandai market each represent different registers of the same tradition. Eating in Pune without trying both is a significant omission.

Ganesh Festival

For eleven days in August or September, Pune belongs entirely to Ganesh Chaturthi — and the transformation of the city is total. The Kasba Ganapati, Tambdi Jogeshwari, and Dagdusheth Halwai mandals have conducted the festival since the 1890s, when Bal Gangadhar Tilak institutionalised it as a cultural mobilisation. The final day's immersion procession is among the most overwhelming public events in India.

Aga Khan Palace

The Aga Khan Palace was built in 1892 as a gift to the poor of the Deccan — but history gave it a different role: Gandhi was interned here during the Quit India Movement, and Kasturba Gandhi and Mahadev Desai both died within its walls. The palace now houses a Gandhi museum and memorial. It is one of the most emotionally resonant sites in Maharashtra.

Tech & Startup Hub

Pune's Hinjewadi IT park, Magarpatta Cybercity, and Balewadi corridors have drawn hundreds of global technology companies and created a startup ecosystem supported by the graduates of IIT Pune, COEP, and the Symbiosis institutes. The city now has a meaningful early-stage venture funding community. For exchange participants interested in technology and entrepreneurship, Pune's size — large enough for serious professional density, small enough to navigate — is a genuine advantage.

Practical Information

Everything you need to know before arriving in Pune.

Accommodation

Options include hostels, host families, and shared flats — ideally within a 4 km radius of your workplace. Your local committee will help arrange everything.

Cost of Living

Expect to spend ₹9,000–12,000/month on food, travel, and daily expenses. The city is affordable by metro standards.

Transportation

Get around via PMPML city buses, Ola/Uber, and auto-rickshaws. Pune also has a growing metro network connecting key areas.

Visa

You'll need a valid Indian visa for your exchange. Your local committee will provide a detailed visa guide and assist with the application process.

Health & Safety

Pune has world-class hospitals including Ruby Hall Clinic and Jehangir Hospital. Your LC will brief you on emergency contacts.

Language

Marathi is the local language but English and Hindi are widely spoken across the city, especially in tech and educational areas.

SIM Card & Internet

Pick up a Jio, Airtel, or Vi SIM at the airport or local mobile store. 4G/5G coverage is excellent city-wide.

Cultural Etiquette

Dress respectfully at temples; festivals are a highlight — join the Ganesh Chaturthi celebrations! Greet with "Namaskar."
